# Problem Mining

<v-clicks>

- The best ideas come from pain, not a whiteboard
- Basecamp: project chaos
- Hootsuite: manage client social accounts
- Content Snare: client content bottleneck
- If you have an idea: ensure repeated problem in your network
- If you don't: reflect & map processes
- delivery, client-side, management, admin, acquisition, marketing, etc. → find repeat, time-costly workflows tech can improve

</v-clicks>

<!--

From all the case-studies, the founders felt the pain *while running their business*—not from a whiteboard.

* 37signals built Basecamp to fix project chaos in their client work
* Invoke Media built BrightKit/Hootsuite to manage client social accounts
* Content Snare came from agencies stuck collecting client content
  * Aktura consistently encountered the same frustrating challenge: getting content and information from clients was a massive bottleneck. James described it as "a total pain in the ass" that "held up payments, stalled entire projects and added all this extra stress we just didn't need".


If you already have an idea or a few, great. Just make sure that it's rooted in a repeated problem that someone in your network has frequently. In the next steps, we'll learn how to validate an idea to actually know if it's a good idea before even writing a line of code.

If you don't have an idea yet, don't worry. Thomas Edison is often quoted as saying "Genius is 1% inspiration and 99% perspiration." The point being that ideas are the easy part, it's the execution that is the bit where things really matter.

Now, to get an idea, I'm going to contradict myself and say go to a whiteboard or better yet ChatGPT. But we're not brainstorming, we're mapping out processes; reflecting.

You as an agency founder have a front-row seat to identify problems in your sector that aren't being solved well or at all. You want to reflect on all the processes within your business (delivery, client-side, management, admin, etc.) and find processes/workflows that are being repeated every so often, that are costly in time, and can be done faster (and better) with technology.
