## 1 - Problem Mining - What to build?

1. Two directions

You shouldn’t pick from the get-go, but its helpful to understand as you dive into problem-searching.

- Automate processes and sell to other agencies
    - Pros:
        - Higher-ticket (generally)
        - Know your prospects through-and-through (you’re the prospect)
    - Cons:
        - Need to create a new acquisition system (from scratch)
- Provide a self-serve platform and sell to your existing customer segment
    - Pros:
        - Leverage your clients as initial users
        - Leverage your current acquisition system
    - Cons:
        - Lower-ticket (generally)
        - More competition

Ok you know that now, great.

From all the case-studies, the founders felt the pain *while running their business*—not from a whiteboard.

- 37signals built Basecamp to fix project chaos in their client work
- Invoke Media built BrightKit/Hootsuite to manage client social accounts
- Content Snare came from agencies stuck collecting client content
    - Aktura consistently encountered the same frustrating challenge: getting content and information from clients was a massive bottleneck. James described it as "a total pain in the ass" that "held up payments, stalled entire projects and added all this extra stress we just didn't need".
- Pixl8 from years of membership-org builds
- Help Scout came from their side-project’s problem for customer support, couldn’t find a good solution (Feed My Inbox)

If you already have an idea or a few, great. Just make sure that it’s rooted in a repeated problem that someone in your network has frequently. In the next steps, we’ll learn how to validate an idea to actually know if it’s a good idea before even writing a line of code.

If you don’t have an idea yet, don’t worry. Thomas Edison is often quoted as saying “Genius is 1% inspiration and 99% perspiration.” The point being that ideas are the easy part, it’s the execution that is the bit where things really matter.

Now, to get an idea, I’m going to contradict myself and say go to a whiteboard or better yet ChatGPT. But we’re not brainstorming, we’re mapping out processes; reflecting.

You as an agency founder have a front-row seat to identify problems in your sector that aren’t being solved well. You want to reflect on all the processes within your business (delivery, client-side, management, admin, etc.) and find processes/workflows that are being repeated every so often, that are costly in time, and can be done faster (and better) with technology.

## 2 - Validation

You have this idea and you think you’re onto something. Now what? Full-force forward and start building? Not just yet.
In case you don’t know what validation is:

Validation is the process of ensuring that a SaaS product solves a real market need (and ideally hint that people will actually pay). Basically, does it have legs? The purpose is to prevent you going down a rabbit hole. The validation process puts your idea as a hypothesis.

The chances that your idea hits the mark perfectly and is a multi-million-dollar idea is extremely low. David Hart’s agency built around 15 products before finally getting to the winner that they’re running today (ScreenCloud).

“Validation Costs You (Almost) Nothing, but Can Save You a Fortune.” Sure—maybe you spend a week or even three validating it. But if you just do the math on the statistics, you’ll save hundreds of thousands of dollars and up to years of wasted time.

So how do you validate?

- Desk Validation
- External Validation

### Desk Validation

While I couldn’t find sources proving that the case-studies *formally* did desk validation, but I imagine every agency owner has the business acumen to do a form of this. Today, it’s just part of the process for SaaS, like a business plan.

A lot of this is preliminary work to see if there’s legs at all. We’ll be cross-checking a lot of this info in the next step of validation.

**Proximity to the Problem**

One of your strongest advantage as an agency owner is having access to all the problems in your own agency and from your clients. I cannot by any means recommend you search for a problem that isn’t faced by yourself or someone in your close network. That personal experience or proximity to customers is crucial. If you can’t directly observe or talk to someone suffering from this problem, trying to figure out if it’s truly a problem worth solving is too much effort and a waste of time.

**Time, Money, Headspace Saved**

Ask yourself, “If my imaginary customer had a magic wand and this solution existed, how much time or money would it save me?”

Quantify it—5 hours per week, or $1,000 per month. Hard figures ground your idea in tangible value which will be helpful later for pricing the SaaS.

Obviously, the more time or money you save them, the more you can charge.

**Head on Fire**

As David puts it: Does the CEO lie awake at night worrying about the
problem you are solving? Or does your product unlock something that helps them solve this problem?”

This, from what I’ve seen and the mistakes we’ve made, is such an underrated point…

We made this mistake…

**Current Solutions**

This one may or may not be possible to do right now by yourself, but if you’re well tied into the problem-solution space as you should be, then you already know how they’re currently solving their problem (maybe its a hacky alternative, maybe its a terrible competitor, etc.). I do not recommend solving problems that you aren’t well versed - after all its one of your biggest strengths as an agency founder, why would you not use it?

One important thing you need to look for and identify early is: How do I know my SaaS will stand out? Your differentiator; your UVP; your moat. What will be unique about your solution that others can’t copy? If you think your advantage is that yours is going to be a bit cheaper or will have a ‘better UI’ than the competition then go back to the idea board. These aren’t strong enough differentiators and are easy to copy.

If you don’t find any competitors… this is both thrilling and suspicious. If you can’t find even a single tool or solution that touches on the same problem, you might be the first genius in the game—or everyone else that’s tried has failed (likely because people wouldn’t pay), so be extremely careful in these cases. If this is the case, look deeply into how customers are already solving this pain point. Because if they’re not, *and* there’s no solution out there that does this, there’s a decently high chance that it’s not worth solving (or you’re on a goldmine).

Help Scout spent two years researching competitors and trying them out. Now, two years is a long time don't get me wrong. But it shows that they really tried figuring out what in the market what works and what doesn't work for them before going into building out their solution.

Practical example from Help Scout:

- Rather than immediately building a solution, Francis spent approximately 2 years researching the customer support market. During this period, they:
    - Tried existing help desk solutions for their own business
    - Found all available options either too complex or impersonal
    - Realized existing solutions turned "customers into numbers" and added friction
    - Identified that small businesses needed something simpler than enterprise solutions

**101 Economics**

This isn’t a crystal-ball, but it’s a start to 1. know if there’s legs 2. establish KPIs we can use if we decide to start.

- How much can you make?
- 1 - Bottom-up Market Sizing
    - **Revenue Potential = Target Customers × Price × Market Penetration %**
    - Price = Value you provide / 5 || Their current annual spending on related tools
    - Example: **Niche Vertical SaaS**
        - **Target Market**: 50,000 companies in specific niche
        - **Pricing**: $299/month ($3,588 annually)
        - **Realistic Penetration**: 8% (easier in focused vertical)
        - **Year 3 Revenue Potential**: $7.32M ARR
        - **Year 5 Revenue Potential**: $13.67M ARR
        - **Potential Valuation (Year 5)**: $68M - $164M
- 2 - TAM Validation
    - **Step 1:** Identify total companies that need your solution
    - **Step 2:** Research their current annual spending on related tools
    - **Step 3:** Estimate what percentage of their budget your solution could capture
    - Example:
        - **B2B Marketing Automation TAM**:
            - Total Market: 2,000,000 companies needing marketing tools
            - Average Annual Marketing Spend: $50,000
            - Your Solution's Share: 2% of marketing budget
            - **Total TAM: $2 Billion**
            - **Realistic Revenue Scenarios**:
                - 0.1% market penetration: $2M ARR
                - 0.5% market penetration: $10M ARR
                - 1.0% market penetration: $20M ARR
                - 2.0% market penetration: $40M ARR
- Trending?
- Cost per customer? (Not so important)

### External Validation

So, to be completely honest, quite a few of the case studies compiled in this playbook didn't use the traditional validation methods that startups use today. After all, the concept was mostly popularized by Eric Lies in 2011 with “The Lean Startup”.

I would say the fundamental reason why they were able to succeed though, was because they were agency owners and they rode the wave. As you are (agency owner) and can do (ride the new AI wave).

Let me break this down a little.

So number one, they were eating their own dog food. They were directly solving a problem that they had every single day and this usually looks like automating a manual process. And this problem was **causing them pain enough** to invest months of their time and invest five to six figures into building a solution.

Secondly, their initial users were their clients. Without these initial users to help them iterate a product I don't think they would make it very far.

And lastly, almost all had developers at hand (or tech co-founders). All they had to do was shift the developers from agency work to product work. And this was a big opportunity cost that they were willing to take on.

So if you don't have engineers at hand, then this is going to be way more expensive than it was for them. So if it costed them, you know, five to six figures and months to years for them, it'll cost you way more.

If you're willing to spend around six figures building a solution for your own problem, *you* know that's a really painful problem. The risk you run is that you're the only one with that problem, or there's not enough people with that exact problem to reach your goals (too small market size). So this is why I emphasized that when you're finding an idea to use your own problem and then in internal validation you should know how many people suffer from this problem, and go the step further and make an educated guesstimate to how many people *really* have their hair on fire from this problem.

I just want to quickly add that as the cost to build software gets cheaper over time, we may get to a point where it's actually more efficient to build an MVP and use itself to validate the problem by getting people to pay. But right now, we're not at the point. I think we're up to a year or two off from that.

**Talking to customers**

This is where it’ll get uncomfortable and that’s totally normal, since we have to go to potential users and talk to them. Let’s start off by admitting something: we prefer to avoid talking to customers about our product ideas before we create them.

Why is that? I think it comes down to three fears:

- not having something tangible to show them
    - the point is them, not your product. actually better you don’t have a product, can’t bias them.
- blowing our one big opportunity to sell:
    - I’ve experienced this firsthand.
    - The prospects we talk to in this phase are likely are “rabid users”; your die-hard users, the ones that will share this with everyone. You don’t care about making money from them.
- Rejection
    - This is the whole point of validation. If you ask a question and they answer something that proves your idea has nothing holding it up - job done, move on.
    - External validation is where you come up with questions that terrify you. And by that I mean questions where the potential answer could kill your idea dead in its tracks.

External validation has been condensed into a pretty rigid science today, I can’t provide much unique value going into specific tactics that you can’t find with ChatGPT (or [my article on validation](https://www.starti.no/blog/how-to-validate-a-saas-idea)) but I’ll very briefly go over the main points, then go into sharing a personal story along with a text-book perfect example of validation from one of the agency-to-saas case-studies.

**Customer Interviews**

Maybe you’ve heard this term thrown around or not, but I’d say the most fundamental part of validation is talking to prospective customers. A shout-out, to the maybe cliche, The Mom Test.

The mum test covers the biggest mistake that people make when doing customer interviews.

Which is they ask the wrong people the wrong questions.

> **You:** Mom, I have a business idea. Do you have 5 minutes?
> 
> 
> **Mom:** Of course, dear.
> 
> *I'm proud of you, and I don’t want to hurt your feelings.*
> 
> **You:** You like your iPad and use it a lot?

>

>

> # **How to Do It Right**
> 
> **You:**
>

> - Is there anything you dislike about them?
> - What was the last cookbook you bought? When? Why?

## 3) MVP **(4/10)**

Build the *smallest* thing that kills the worst blocker.

One of the most common mistakes is not making it lean enough.

Content Snare: "We rolled too many things into the first version which blew out our development budget. We spent way too much on our MVP".

Our job as SaaS engineers for our clients is to always push on less.

Your ambition works against you in this stage, your vision is grand - yet right now we need something minimal. It’s so common that you build something and end up realizing the real value for users is slightly different than expected.

We want to mold the product *according to the user; deconstructive vs constructive art*

## 4) 0 → 10 Evangelists

Almost all of the agency-to-saas case-studies got their first dollar from existing clients and network. People buy from people they like, and they like you (or at least I hope).

I will confidently say alongside every other successful SaaS founder out there, that getting your first 10 paid users is the hardest part (which is why we stick with our partners until that 10th paying customers). Getting 10 paid users proves your product is solving a problem, sure you’re not at product-market-fit yet, but you know you have something. You’re no longer in the dark.

If you’ve done it right, these 10 first users are your evangelists - they’ll be the catalyst to the flywheel that’s bound to come your way. By done it right, I mean focusing on these couple handful of users like they’re your only customers. Get them the results they need, build the features they need, etc. At scale, people behave similarly. The chances are (depending on your market size), these 10 users represent your entire market’s behavior. So essentially you’re iterating your product to perfection for the entire market through these 10.

## 5) Monetization

So this step is very flexible. In terms of pricing the actual product itself, you'll learn over time how much value people are getting out of it and you can update your assumptions from the Desk Validation. But in terms of where you place this SaaS in your entire offer from your agency, there's a couple different ideas.

OmniFusion AI, they build AI agents for sales and outbound, they added the SaaS as part of their value ladder. So for a low-ticket offer, they offered their SaaS for a couple hundred a month. And then they got into education. And at the top of the tier is their services, their custom services. This is also what Pixl8 did.

Another option is to offer this SaaS completely separate to your services if your selling to other agencies instead of your customers.

If your product is doing something new that you don’t offer as a service, maybe you can add it as an up-sell.

## 6) GTM Engine: pick one primary, one secondary

Now, for marketing and sales, there are *so* many different ways that you can get your product out there. I'm talking about beyond the first 10 users. We're trying to grow.

I would say this really depends on what existing skill sets and assets you already have and leveraging those. And then in some edge cases, there's some particular channel that might work better for others. But generally, for almost every type of SaaS product, if you just pick one where your customers are (gen Z isn’t on email, boomers aren’t on social media), nail it down, and keep doing that.

- **Mailchimp**
    - Freemium + viral footer + referral bounty; paired with how-to content for SMBs.
    - Self-serve onboarding → huge top-of-funnel; content nurtured upgrades.
- **Basecamp (37signals)**
    - Audience-first via Signal v. Noise + opinionated positioning; zero ads.
    - Simple pricing and UX → word-of-mouth inside design/agency circles.
- **Hootsuite**
    - Free public tool → PR/tech-press/influencer lift; then freemium upgrades.
    - Education (Hootsuite Academy) to mature users into paid/enterprise.
- **Proposify**
    - SEO + webinars/podcasts on proposals/sales ops; integrations (CRM/e-sign) as channels.
    - Started self-serve, added sales-assist to move upmarket.
- **Content Snare**
    - Niche communities (FB groups) + SEO on “collecting client content.”
    - Pre-sold cohort became evangelists; steady referrals within agencies/accounting.
- **nDash**
    - Two-sided marketplace flywheel (writers ↔ brands) + content marketing.
    - Founder-led outreach to seed brands; community growth drives inbound.
- **EditCloud**
    - Enterprise pilots → ROI case studies (productivity/cost) as sales ammo.
    - Relationship-driven sales (leveraging founder and investor networks)
- **Pixl8 (ReadyMembership/Eventfolio)**
    - Deep solution content and thought leadership (presentations, white papers, live demos
    at client events)

## 7) Funding

As an agency, you’re well positioned to fund a project yourself out of savings and profits, which is what how most of the case-studies did it. Whether you decide to take on external funding depends on how fast and how big you want to go. But at least you keep the most valuable initial equity bootstrapping it.

- **Basecamp (37signals)** — Client-services agency (1999) → built Basecamp (2003–04) → bootstrapped profitability → 2006 minority, no-control secondary sale to Jeff Bezos (no operating capital raised) → continued profit-funded growth.
- **Mailchimp (The Rocket Science Group)** — Web agency (2001) → product spin-out (2001–02) → stayed bootstrapped ~20 years (no VC) → acquired by Intuit for ~$12B cash+stock (2021).

## 8) Focus Decision: keep agency, spin out, or cut the cord

But the lesson that David Hart stresses really hard is that at some point, you need to decide whether you're an agency with a SaaS product or you're a SaaS company with a agency arm.

And one of them has to be your side project, because as you know, as a business owner, you need focus and you need focus to push something to the scale that you want it to. With your agency, you put all the focus and you got it to a seven-figure business. And now, when your SaaS is starting to scale, you're going to need to decide how and when you make this transition.

"Splitting our focus between running a successful agency and trying to build a fledgeling Proposify hurt us, and we needed to shit or get off the proverbial pot," Racki said.

- **Clean break** **(3/10):** Basecamp shut the agency once product revenue crossed agency’s; Proposify sold the agency; nDash ultimately pivoted off agency (caution: did it a bit too soon → cash squeeze "pivoted off the agency model a bit too soon”).
- **Spin-out** **(1/10):** Hootsuite spun out; Invoke continued separately.
- **Hybrid (SaaS + services) long-term** **(3/10):** Pixl8 intentionally hybridized; EditCloud kept consultative layer;

## TL;DR — The winning route

1. Find a problem in your agency.
2. Validate through desk research and customer interviews.
3. Build the **smallest** possible MVP.
4. Get 10 evangelists from your network who cannot live without your product.
5. Choose a monetization strategy that complements your agency (separate product, value ladder, etc.).
6. Build one primary GTM channel (usually content/education).
7. Leverage agency cash flow initially; consider outside funding only when product has proven traction.
8. Make the hard decision: keep agency, spin out SaaS, or go all-in on SaaS.
